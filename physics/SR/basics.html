Basics of Special Relativity

Special relativity is the kinematic framework that replaces Galilean (Newtonian) spacetime once one demands that the speed of light be the same in every inertial frame. It governs the structure of spacetime and the transformation laws of energy, momentum, and fields. Throughout we work in an inertial frame and frequently adopt natural units c=1, restoring factors of c where useful.

The Two Postulates

Einstein's 1905 formulation rests on two postulates:

  1. Principle of relativity. The laws of physics take the same form in all inertial frames. No experiment can single out a preferred inertial frame or detect absolute uniform motion.
  2. Invariance of the speed of light. Light propagates in vacuum with the same speed c in every inertial frame, independent of the motion of the source or observer.

The second postulate is the radical one: it forces simultaneity, time intervals, and lengths to become frame-dependent.

Spacetime and the Invariant Interval

Events are points in four-dimensional spacetime, labeled by coordinates xμ=(ct,x,y,z) with μ=0,1,2,3. The central invariant is the spacetime interval between two events,

Δs2=c2Δt2−Δx2−Δy2−Δz2,

which all inertial observers agree on. In differential form,

ds2=c2dt2−dx2−dy2−dz2=ημν​dxμdxν,

where the Minkowski metric (with the "mostly minus" convention) is

ημν​=diag(+1,−1,−1,−1).

Intervals are classified by their sign:

Lorentz Transformations

The transformations between inertial frames that preserve the interval are Lorentz transformations. For a boost with velocity v along the x-axis, write β=v/c and the Lorentz factor

γ=1−β2​1​≥1.

The coordinates transform as

ct′x′y′​=γ(ct−βx),=γ(x−βct),=y,z′=z.​

In matrix form, x′μ=Λμν​xν with

Λ=​γ−γβ00​−γβγ00​0010​0001​​.

The full set of transformations preserving ημν​ (boosts plus spatial rotations) forms the Lorentz group O(1,3); adjoining spacetime translations gives the Poincaré group, the full symmetry group of special-relativistic physics.

Kinematic Consequences

Three classic effects follow directly from the Lorentz transformations.

Time Dilation

A clock moving at speed v runs slow relative to the frame in which it is observed:

Δt=γΔτ,

where Δτ is the proper time measured in the clock's rest frame.

Length Contraction

An object of rest length L0​ moving at speed v along its length is measured to be shorter:

L=γL0​​.

Relativistic Velocity Addition

Velocities along the boost direction combine as

u′=1−uv/c2u−v​,

which guarantees that no combination of subluminal speeds exceeds c, and that light always travels at c.

Four-Vectors

Quantities that transform like xμ under Lorentz transformations are four-vectors. The two most important are:

  • Four-position: xμ=(ct,x).
  • Four-momentum: pμ=(E/c,p).

The Minkowski inner product of a four-vector with itself is Lorentz-invariant. For the four-momentum,

pμ​pμ=(cE​)2−∣p∣2=(mc)2,

where m is the invariant (rest) mass.

Relativistic Energy and Momentum

For a particle of rest mass m moving with velocity v (and γ computed from v=∣v∣):

E=γmc2,p=γmv.

At rest (γ=1) this gives the famous rest energy

E=mc2.

In the non-relativistic limit v≪c, expanding γ recovers

E≈mc2+21​mv2,

the rest energy plus the Newtonian kinetic energy.

The Energy–Momentum Dispersion Relation

Eliminating the velocity from E=γmc2 and p=γmv — equivalently, reading off the invariant norm of pμ — gives the relativistic dispersion relation:

E2=(pc)2+(mc2)2.

Special cases:

  • Massless particles (m=0): E=pc, the linear dispersion of light, ω=c∣k∣.
  • Non-relativistic limit (p≪mc): E≈mc2+2mp2​.

This relation is a direct consequence of Lorentz invariance and is the form that reappears in quantum field theory as the on-shell condition p2=m2 (in natural units) — the pole of the free propagator. Promoting E and p to the operators i∂t​ and −i∇ acting on a wavefunction turns this relation into the Klein–Gordon equation; see QFT/theories/QED/historical.md § 1.1.
